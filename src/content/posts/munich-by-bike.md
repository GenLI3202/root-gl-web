There's a stretch of the Isar between Thalkirchen and Flaucher where the path drops close to the water and the city disappears. You're in a river valley, light filtering through poplars, and for a few minutes Munich is just sound — tyres on gravel, wind, birdsong. Then you climb back up and you're in Sendling, locked in traffic, and the spell breaks.

I've ridden that stretch maybe two hundred times. It still gets me every time.

## Starting the Club

I joined [Accross Cycling Club](https://accross-cc.de) almost by accident. A friend dragged me to a Wednesday evening ride, I showed up on a flat-bar commuter bike, and kept up with people on carbon. By the following week I'd booked a weekend ride. Two months later I was helping plan routes.

Managing a club teaches you things no PhD program covers. You learn that "Saturday 8am, Marienplatz" sounds simple until forty people show up with incompatible expectations about pace. You learn that the person who complains the loudest about the route is also the first to offer to carry the spare inner tubes. You learn that group dynamics on a bike are weirdly honest — you can't hide effort at 30 km/h uphill.

Mostly you learn that the logistics are just the container. The thing people actually come for is the conversation at the café stop, the shared grimace on the climb, the specific satisfaction of having been somewhere under your own power.

## What Bikes Are For

I've come to believe that bikes are fundamentally a *pace* technology. Cars are too fast — you're in a metal box, disconnected, the city is a blur. Walking is too slow for anything beyond the immediate neighbourhood. Bikes hit the sweet spot: fast enough to cover ground, slow enough to notice things.

I've found bakeries, bookshops, canal paths, and a surprisingly good Vietnamese restaurant purely by bike, in the sense that I would have missed them in a car and never reached them on foot. A city you only know from the U-Bahn and Google Maps is a city of destinations. A city you know by bike is a city of *between*.

## Munich in Winter

The received wisdom is that Munich winters make cycling impractical. This is approximately 40% true. The other 60% is that winter rides, when you do them, are extraordinary. The English Garden at 7am on a cold February morning, frost on the grass, almost no one there — that's a version of the city most residents never see.

The key is layering, fenders, and adjusting your expectations. You're not going fast. You're going somewhere on a machine using only your own energy, and in January that's actually remarkable enough.
