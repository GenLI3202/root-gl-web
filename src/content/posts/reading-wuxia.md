The first time I read *The Smiling Proud Wanderer* (笑傲江湖), I was twelve and reading it for the swordfights. The second time, twenty years later, I was reading it for Linghu Chong's relationship to his own practice — his refusal of orthodoxy, his intuitive style that horrifies his master, his eventual discovery that freedom requires abandoning the very techniques he spent years mastering.

It hit differently as a PhD student.

## The Problem of the Technique

Wuxia novels are full of a specific kind of tragedy: the practitioner who becomes so committed to a single technique that they can't grow beyond it. Master Ren Woxing in the same novel is a genius who discovers an immensely powerful internal energy method — and spends the last third of the book dying because he can't stop using it. The power is real. The limitation is that it has become the only thing he knows how to do.

This maps, uncomfortably well, onto a problem in academic research. You spend years developing expertise in a method — stochastic programming, say, or a specific simulation framework — and then you start seeing every problem as a nail for your particular hammer. The technique is genuine expertise. The pathology is assuming it's the only relevant kind.

## What Mastery Actually Looks Like

Jin Yong's ideal practitioners are almost always *integrators*. Zhang Wuji in *The Heaven Sword and Dragon Saber* becomes powerful not by perfecting one style but by learning enough different systems to perceive their underlying principles. The Dugu Qiubai figure (Lonely Solitary) reaches the ultimate level and finds that the best sword technique is no fixed technique at all.

This is also, I think, what good interdisciplinary research looks like. Not abandoning depth, but using depth in one area to understand the structure of problems in adjacent ones. The operations research lens helps me see energy systems problems as resource allocation under uncertainty. The energy systems context gives the operations research problems their actual stakes.

The synthesis is harder to publish than either component alone. It's also more interesting.

## On Liu Cixin vs Jin Yong

I've read both extensively and they satisfy completely different needs. Liu Cixin (*The Three-Body Problem*) gives you the vertigo of scale — civilizational stakes, deep time, the universe as a problem to be solved. Jin Yong gives you the texture of individual lives within larger structures — how people navigate loyalty, love, institutional corruption, and the limits of their own characters.

If I'm being honest, I come back to Jin Yong more often. *The Deer and the Cauldron* alone — a novel whose protagonist is explicitly *not* a hero, who succeeds precisely because he refuses the heroic codes everyone around him takes seriously — is one of the most quietly subversive books I've read in any language.

The best science fiction and the best wuxia are both, underneath everything, asking: *what do humans do with power?* They just answer from different directions.
